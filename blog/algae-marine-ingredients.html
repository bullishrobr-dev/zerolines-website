Brown Algae and Red Algae: Marine Ingredients That Actually Work

March 26, 2026 6 min read

Marine algae have been around for over 3 billion years. They've survived mass extinctions, ice ages, and dramatic shifts in atmospheric chemistry. To do that, they evolved a remarkable arsenal of protective compounds — UV filters, antioxidants, osmoprotectants, and anti-inflammatory molecules. Many of these compounds translate directly to human skin benefits.

Brown Algae: The Exfoliation Expert

Brown algae (Phaeophyceae) are the largest and most complex marine algae. They include kelp, wakame, and bladderwrack — organisms that can grow up to 60 metres in length. For skincare, we use brown algae extracts for their enzymatic and bioactive properties.

The key compound is a group of enzymes called polysaccharide hydrolases. These enzymes break down the bonds between dead skin cells — the same mechanism that chemical exfoliants use, but gentler. Brown algae extract exfoliates without the acidity of AHAs or the irritation of physical scrubs.

Brown algae are also rich in fucoidan, a sulphated polysaccharide with documented anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. Fucoidan has been shown to inhibit MMPs — the enzymes that break down collagen — making it valuable for ageing skin.

We use brown algae extract in our Bio-Renewal Peeling Gel, where it provides gentle enzymatic exfoliation alongside aloe vera soothing and Pyrenean mineral water hydration.

Red Algae: The UV Protector

Red algae (Rhodophyta) live in deeper water than brown algae, where blue and green light penetrate but UV is filtered. Despite this, they've evolved potent UV-protective compounds called mycosporine-like amino acids (MAAs). These are natural sunscreens that absorb UVB and UVA radiation without generating free radicals.

Red algae are also rich in phycoerythrin, a red pigment with powerful antioxidant activity. Studies show it scavenges free radicals more effectively than vitamin C in some assays. And they're a significant source of taurine, an amino acid that supports skin hydration and barrier function.

We use red algae extract in our Environmental Shield Day Cream, where it complements mineral UV filters with additional photoprotective compounds.

Why Marine Ingredients Work

The evolutionary pressure on marine algae is intense. They can't escape their environment — they must adapt to it. The result is compounds that are:

  • Highly stable: Developed to function in salt water, high pressure, and variable light
  • Multi-functional: Often serving several protective roles simultaneously
  • Biocompatible: Evolved in aqueous environments, making them compatible with human skin
  • Novel: Many marine compounds have no terrestrial equivalent

Green Algae: The Hydration Specialist

Green algae (Chlorophyta) are often overlooked in favour of their brown and red cousins, but they offer distinct advantages for skin hydration. Chlorella and Ulva lactuca (sea lettuce) contain unique polysaccharides called ulvans that form a moisture-retentive film on the skin surface without occlusivity.

Unlike silicones that sit on top of the skin, green algae polysaccharides integrate with the stratum corneum's natural lipid matrix. They attract and bind water molecules within the skin's upper layers, increasing hydration without blocking the pores or interfering with the skin's respiratory functions. Clinical studies show a 23% increase in skin hydration after four weeks of topical green algae extract use.

Sustainability Considerations

Not all marine ingredients are harvested sustainably. Some kelp forests are being depleted by industrial harvesting. At Zero Lines, we source our algae extracts from certified sustainable aquaculture operations — farmed, not wild-harvested — that operate without damaging marine ecosystems.

The extracts are produced using green chemistry methods: aqueous extraction, no harsh solvents, minimal waste. The biomass is cultivated in controlled conditions that ensure consistent quality and eliminate contamination risk from ocean pollutants.

Future Directions in Marine Skincare

Marine biotechnology is advancing rapidly. Researchers are exploring extremophiles — organisms that live in extreme conditions like deep-sea hydrothermal vents and Antarctic ice — for compounds with unique protective properties. These organisms have evolved mechanisms to survive temperature extremes, high pressure, and intense radiation that dwarf anything human skin faces.

We're monitoring this research closely and have already identified several promising compounds for future formulation. The ocean remains the largest unexplored reservoir of bioactive molecules on Earth, and we've only scratched the surface of what's possible.
