Niacinamide: The One Ingredient Everyone Over 30 Should Know

March 19, 2026 6 min read

If you could only use one active ingredient for the rest of your life, niacinamide would be a strong contender. It's not the most exciting ingredient — no dramatic peeling, no tingling sensation, no overnight transformation. But it is arguably the most versatile, the best tolerated, and the most comprehensively researched.

What Niacinamide Actually Is

Niacinamide is the amide form of vitamin B3 (niacin). Unlike niacin, it doesn't cause flushing at high doses. It's water-soluble, stable in heat and light, and compatible with most other skincare ingredients — a formulator's dream.

In skin, niacinamide is converted to NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide), a coenzyme that plays a central role in cellular energy metabolism. Without adequate NAD+, skin cells can't function optimally. With it, they repair faster, produce more collagen, and maintain better barrier integrity.

What It Does: The Complete List

Niacinamide is unusual in that it has multiple well-documented mechanisms, not just one:

1. Strengthens the Barrier

Niacinamide increases the production of ceramides, free fatty acids, and cholesterol — the three components of the skin's lipid barrier. Studies show a 2% concentration improves barrier function within 4 weeks. A stronger barrier means less sensitivity, less dehydration, and better resilience against environmental stressors.

2. Regulates Sebum

At concentrations of 2–4%, niacinamide reduces sebum production by normalising the activity of sebaceous glands. This isn't drying — it's regulatory. Skin becomes less oily without becoming tight or dehydrated.

3. Minimises Pores

Pores appear larger when they're clogged with sebum and dead skin cells. By reducing sebum and improving skin elasticity, niacinamide makes pores appear smaller over time. It's not actually changing pore size (that's genetically determined), but it changes how they look — which is what most people care about.

4. Brightens and Evens Tone

Niacinamide inhibits the transfer of melanin from melanocytes to keratinocytes. This reduces hyperpigmentation, dark spots, and uneven tone without the irritation or rebound pigmentation that hydroquinone can cause. Studies show visible brightening after 8–12 weeks of consistent use at 4–5%.

5. Supports Collagen

Niacinamide stimulates collagen synthesis and inhibits glycation — the process by which sugar molecules damage collagen fibres. This dual action makes it particularly valuable for ageing skin.

6. Reduces Inflammation

Niacinamide has documented anti-inflammatory effects, making it suitable for rosacea, acne, and sensitive skin. It reduces redness without the thinning or fragility that corticosteroids cause.

The Optimal Concentration

Research shows that 2–5% niacinamide is the sweet spot. Below 2%, effects are minimal. Above 5%, there's no additional benefit and some risk of irritation in sensitive individuals. The 5% concentration used in our Renewal and Repair Night Cream is at the top of the effective range — maximum benefit without unnecessary risk.

Compatibility: The Ultimate Team Player

Niacinamide works with virtually everything:

  • With vitamin C: The old advice to separate them has been debunked. They work synergistically.
  • With retinol: Niacinamide reduces the irritation that retinol causes, making them an excellent pair.
  • With AHAs/BHAs: Niacinamide supports barrier repair after exfoliation.
  • With peptides: Both support collagen through different mechanisms.
  • With hyaluronic acid: Niacinamide strengthens the barrier; hyaluronic acid hydrates. Complementary functions.

Who Should Use It

Short answer: almost everyone. Niacinamide is suitable for all skin types, all ages, and all concerns. It's particularly valuable if you're:

  • Over 30 and noticing the first signs of ageing
  • Dealing with enlarged pores or oily skin
  • Struggling with hyperpigmentation or uneven tone
  • Experiencing sensitivity or barrier impairment
  • Building a multi-step routine and need an ingredient that plays well with others

Niacinamide: The Multi-Tasking Molecule

Niacinamide, also known as vitamin B3 or nicotinamide, is one of the most well-researched ingredients in skincare. Unlike retinoids or acids, it doesn't work by irritating the skin into repair mode. It works by supporting the skin's own processes — making it exceptionally well-tolerated, even by sensitive skin.

At the cellular level, niacinamide is a precursor to NAD+ and NADP+, coenzymes involved in hundreds of metabolic reactions. In skin specifically, it supports barrier repair by stimulating ceramide synthesis, reduces inflammation by inhibiting cytokine release, regulates sebum production, and inhibits melanin transfer from melanocytes to keratinocytes — reducing hyperpigmentation.

Concentration Matters

Niacinamide is effective across a wide concentration range, but 2–5% is the sweet spot for most concerns. At 2%, it significantly improves barrier function. At 4–5%, it shows measurable effects on pore appearance, fine lines, and uneven tone. Concentrations above 10% don't provide additional benefits and can cause flushing in some individuals.
